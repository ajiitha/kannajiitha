Reflections of my own 💭 (for all my connections)

Reflection on Oversharing:

I've recently come to realize that my perspective on oversharing wasn't entirely accurate. While I used to believe that it was merely a matter of sharing with the right people, I now understand that it's more nuanced than that. Oversharing isn't just about the audience; it's about the nature and amount of information being shared.

Authenticity and Boundaries:

I used to equate authenticity with full disclosure, not realizing that being genuine doesn't mean sharing every detail of my life. I now see that oversharing can overwhelm others, especially if they are not prepared for a substantial amount of personal information. True authenticity involves striking a balance between openness and respecting boundaries.

Having gained these insights, I am committed to working on this part of myself. I want my communication to be thoughtful, considerate, and respectful of others' boundaries. I only caqre for genuine connections based on mutual understanding and shared experiences, rather than confusing oversharing for authenticity/intimacy.

Moving Forward: I believe that by being more mindful of what and how I share, I can contribute to healthier and more authentic relationships. I want to create spaces where everyone feels comfortable and respected, fostering genuine connections built on trust and understanding.

Some Notes:

He is not the only one threatening to leave during conflict. I did it too. By telling him "if you react this way again, I will leave" or "I can't do this" is a threat!! We both threaten the relationship out of fear of abandonment, fear of losing each other.

He threatens to leave before he’s left.

I threaten to leave him if he doesn’t change. I did not realize I was also threatening the relationship. Both threats are fear-based. We don't actually want to leave. We just don't want to get hurt.

Saying “two strikes” to him is a threat. I can't do that.

In the heat of the moment, we don’t try to listen and help each other with our attachment styles and our fear of losing each other. Analogy - anger is a child - both our children are fighting. The adults return after the fight and we know what to say. Take a moment to calm down, remove ourselves from the situation before we say things we don't mean. Reconvene and talk as adults.

Three things you never do in an argument with your partner:

1) No name calling, ever.

2) Do not say "always" or "never" example: "you always do this, you never do that!"

3) Never threaten the relationship (which is what we are facing) threatening to leave, threatening with the gold chain.

When we start off at the North Pole (on top of the world), everywhere else is South. The fall from a pedestal is long and hard. Acknowledge we are both humans. We are both flawed. But flaws and conflict does not mean the end. It just means we are human and we need to work together for change because change does not happen right away.

We are a team, we have to remind each other that conflicts does not mean it's the end. It's a chance to learn, observe and do better. We are still a new couple, it will take time as long as we can be patient with each other because we are worth it.

So those are some notes and key points I learned from today's session which we'll talk about in person too. I just like writing here when we can't see each other hehe. Kato was very happy to hear I found you. She's even happier I came to her with this to tackle early on.
